How many 'M's are there in money? Just one? Perhaps not

Because there are different types of deposits, there are different ways that economists think of what money is.

Three definitions often used are 'M1', 'M3' (there you are two Ms!) and 'Broad Money'. All these definitions include notes and coins in your hands, so that's always money! However, M1 only includes current or cheque deposits with trading banks (which allow quick and easy withdrawals). M3 includes current and term deposits with all banks. Broad Money includes current and term deposits with all banks (not including money held in non-bank financial institutions) plus borrowings by these organisations.

The majority of money is in the form of deposits... not notes and coins! So if everyone in the economy were to withdraw their money from a bank at the same time, this would be known as a 'run on the bank' and the bank would run out of its cash reserves and go out of business!

Trading Banks

These are banks that do not usually provide services to the general public. Trading banks provide lines of credit to businesses and get collateral in return.

Term Deposits

A term deposit is when you give some money to the bank to hold for a fixed amount of time - usually six months or more. You will get higher interest on this deposit, compared to money that is in your savings account, but you can't get your money back until the contract has ended.

Non-Bank Financial Institutions

Non-Bank Financial Institutions are organisations like credit unions, insurance firms or currency exchanges. These institutions do not have full banking licences and are not regulated by banking regulatory agencies.
