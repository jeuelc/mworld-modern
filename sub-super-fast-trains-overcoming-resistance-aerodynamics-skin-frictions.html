Skin Friction

 
click here

Air is sticky. Really! When it acts over the entire surface of a super-fast train that's 200 m long, this becomes really important. The stickiness of a gas or liquid is called 'viscosity': Air has low viscosity, honey has high viscosity.

If you were trying to drive a train through honey, the resistance would be much greater than air. (It would taste pretty good if you stuck your finger out the window though!) The stickiness of the honey would make it harder for the train to slide through it. This is called skin friction.

Skin friction exists because of something called the 'no slip condition'. Air that is close to the train gets dragged along; it can't slip over it. Further away from the surface, the air is dragged less and less, until eventually it isn't dragged at all.

The region of dragged air near the surface is called the 'boundary layer'. To reduce skin friction, you want the smallest amount of air being dragged possible, so the surface of the train has to be as smooth as you can possibly get it!
