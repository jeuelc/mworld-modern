Pressure Drag

When a super-fast train is driving on a calm day, it is running into still air so the nose of the train experiences high pressure. Like when you run really fast, you can feel the pressure from the wind on your face!

The train punches a hole through the still air as it travels, so that behind the tail there is disturbed, turbulent air known as the train's wake. The air is turbulent because the air flow has separated from the train near the tail and is no longer moving smoothly over the surface. We call this 'flow separation'. Where the flow has separated, low pressure exists, so there is low pressure on the trains tail.

Because there is high pressure on the nose, and low pressure on the tail, the result is a force that acts against the direction the train is travelling; this is pressure drag.
