Fossil Fuels and the Atmosphere

Coal, oil and natural gas provide the fuel for most of the cars, trains, planes, buses and power stations on the Earth. We call these fossil fuels because they were formed from the fossils of plants and animals that died a long time ago.

Fossil fuels emit various chemicals into the Earth's atmosphere when they are burned. The atmosphere is vital to life on Earth. It protects the Earth from cosmic rays, meteors and other objects from outer space. It also acts like a blanket and traps some of the heat that is reflected from the Earth, keeping the Earth much warmer than Mars, for example.

If we emit too many chemicals into the atmosphere then the blanket effect might increase and make the Earth warmer than it is now. If this happens, ice in Antarctica and on land near the Arctic would melt and lead to rises in the levels of the world's oceans. A warmer Earth might have other serious effects, such as an increase in severe storms and making it harder to grow food in some places.
